# Claims Matrix

This claims matrix maps key scientific claims in clinical machine learning literature to their empirical evidence, limitations, and how the AETHEL auditing framework improves upon them.

| Paper | Claim | Evidence | Limitation | How AETHEL Improves Upon It | Citation Location |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Guo et al. (2017)**<br>*On calibration of modern neural networks* | Post-hoc calibration scaling (e.g., Platt scaling, Temperature scaling) restores probability calibration. | Reduces Expected Calibration Error (ECE) to near zero on validation splits of vision and NLP tasks. | Assumes IID data; does not address calibration drift under domain or covariate shift. | AETHEL applies post-hoc Platt scaling and Isotonic regression specifically under demographic/cohort transfer shifts, demonstrating recovery of 78.8% of calibration loss under shift. | Section 3.2 (Calibration Under Shift) |
| **Steyerberg et al. (2010)**<br>*Assessing the performance of prediction models* | Decision Curve Analysis (DCA) Net Benefit measures clinical utility better than statistical accuracy (AUC). | Integrates decision threshold probability as a cost-benefit ratio to calculate clinical Net Benefit. | Abstract Net Benefit curves are difficult for clinical staff to interpret and apply to direct patient care decisions. | AETHEL translates DCA Net Benefit curves into a bedside 100-cell consequence waffle chart, displaying concrete numbers of true/false positives per 1,000 patients. | Section 4.3 (Clinical Utility Analysis) |
| **Lundberg & Lee (2017)**<br>*A unified approach to interpreting model predictions* | SHAP values provide local feature attributions satisfying local accuracy, missingness, and consistency. | Proves that game-theory Shapley values define the unique class of additive feature attribution methods. | Local attributions are unstable and can shift significantly when models are transferred across cohorts. | AETHEL formulates the Feature Agreement Score (FAS) and Jaccard Top-k Overlap (TkO) to measure and audit explanation consensus across cohorts. | Section 3.4 (Feature Attribution Stability) |
| **Subbaswamy et al. (2019)**<br>*Preventing database shift failures in clinical ML* | Covariate and distribution shifts lead to silent model failures, requiring algorithmic interventions. | Proves bounds on database shift failures and designs algorithms to mitigate them. | Relies heavily on prior knowledge of causal graphs, which are rarely known in clinical practice. | AETHEL implements empirical, data-driven stress testing via continuous Gaussian noise sweeps and binary missingness sweeps to map model failure surfaces. | Section 4.1 (Robustness Audit Boundaries) |
| **Ter-Minassian et al. (2023)**<br>*Explainable AI for survival analysis* | Standard mean-based SHAP values are biased in censored survival analysis; median-SHAP resolves this bias. | Proves divergence of mean SHAP under censoring and demonstrates median-SHAP on time-to-event outcomes. | Median-SHAP has high computational overhead and lacks consensus verification against model-native importances. | AETHEL integrates multi-method consensus metrics (FAS) to validate explanation stability across various importance methods. | Section 3.4 (Feature Attribution Stability) |
| **Collins et al. (2024)**<br>*TRIPOD+AI statement* | Clinical predictive modeling using machine learning requires transparent reporting of dataset specs and parameters. | Consensus reporting guidelines developed by international Delphi consensus panels. | Checking compliance is manual and retrospective, with no software integration during the development cycle. | AETHEL automates reporting compliance by linking nested cross-validation (LeakageFreeCV) and training logs directly to LaTeX table generation. | Methods / Discussion |
| **Brankovic et al. (2023)**<br>*Evaluation of Popular XAI Applied to Clinical Models* | Popular local XAI methods (SHAP, LIME) produce inconsistent feature importances, eroding clinical trust. | Measures pairwise correlations and feature overlap of explanations on clinical models. | Identifies inconsistency as a vulnerability but lacks an active auditing framework to track it across transfer cohorts. | AETHEL incorporates explanation consistency metrics (FAS/TkO) into an active, cohort-level auditing dashboard. | Section 3.4 (Feature Attribution Stability) |
| **Zadrozny & Elkan (2001)**<br>*Obtaining calibrated probability estimates* | Post-hoc Platt scaling and Isotonic regression correct calibration of decision trees and naive Bayes. | Evaluates calibration on public KDD datasets, showing substantial Brier score improvements. | Evaluated only on stable, single-source datasets with no transfer shift. | AETHEL validates these methods on clinical cohorts under transfer shift, showing that Isotonic regression is highly effective under low shift but Platt scaling is more robust under large shift. | Section 3.2 (Calibration Under Shift) |
| **Ribeiro et al. (2016)**<br>*“Why Should I Trust You?”* | Local interpretable model-agnostic explanations (LIME) explain individual predictions of any classifier. | Fits local sparse linear models around perturbed inputs, showing human trust increases. | LIME is highly sensitive to the perturbation scale and kernel, causing explanation instability. | AETHEL audits explanation stability across seeds and cohorts, comparing LIME to SHAP and Permutation importances to expose LIME's variance. | Section 3.4 (Feature Attribution Stability) |
| **Riley et al. (2021)**<br>*Minimum sample size for external validation* | Calculates minimum sample sizes for clinical prediction model validation to guarantee precision. | Formulates closed-form mathematical equations based on target precision of AUC and calibration slope. | Requires estimated event rates of target cohorts, which may not align with actual EHR collections. | AETHEL implements automated sample size auditing, verifying event counts (events > 200) in target Framingham/NHANES cohorts before auditing. | Methods / Limitations |
